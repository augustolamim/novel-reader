Snow was falling.

No, "falling" was putting it too mildly. This was white violence fit to be called a heavy blizzard. Every single flake dropping from the sky carried a striking weight, like it was battering us and stealing away our body heat.

Climbing snowy mountains on the way to the highest peak on the continent was a little rough.

I forgot the exact elevation, but it looked to be higher than Everest without even trying. The surrounding mountains were high too, and it didn't help that it took so long just to reach the sacred peak Mont Celerune.

Since this was the place closest to the heavens in the world, I supposed snow was only natural. If anything, it would have been stranger if it didn't snow.

My field of vision was constantly painted white, my breath came out white, and before I knew it my eyelashes had frozen from my own breath.

A white shape slid across a distant ridgeline.

A white frost dragon--at least, that was the provisional name I'd given it. Dragons sometimes flew through the blizzard half-hidden by the snow. Scales that blended into the storm, wings clear as ice. They moved through the sky at such a distance that I couldn't even tell whether they'd noticed us.

Maybe they had nests here. Or maybe the mountain itself was dragon territory.

Maybe that was the sort of thing that made people call it a sacred peak and stay away.

We had done nothing but keep walking for days.

It had been a long enough march that I honestly found myself missing human settlements. I wanted a meal from a food stall or a restaurant.

That was impossible, though. There wasn't a trace of humanity here, or even of civilization. All there was were monsters stronger than those found elsewhere, and beasts that had adapted to this land and grown huge. This place was completely outside the world of man. And at the top of that ecosystem, presumably, were dragons.

It was a lonely road. You could call it that.

If I'd come alone, it would have been an ascetic ordeal of trudging wordlessly through white silence ruled by nothing but footsteps and wind.

Well, I wasn't actually alone, because my partner Lumiela was with me.

"Hey, if we followed that dragon and found its nest, do you think there'd be treasure or something?"

With my head full of distractions, I thought of how dragon nests meant treasure, maybe, and said it out loud without thinking.

"After ruins, now it's dragon nests? That's a new kind of treasure hunt. We're not rushing anywhere, and dragon nests sound dependable. Let's raid a few on the way."

Walking beside me, Lumiela replied while gazing in the direction the dragon had flown.

It was nice just having an answer come back. There wasn't much out here to enjoy besides simple verbal back-and-forth.

Both Lumiela and I were bundled up in fluffy winter gear. I had pulled another layer on over my usual suspicious cloak, and the result was fashionably dead on arrival.

Apparently it was made from the fur of some monster that lived in cold regions or something, and its heat retention was supposed to be excellent.

Lumiela, too, had it on, with a scarf wrapped around and around her neck, so the only thing still spirit-of-light-like about her now was the little bit of golden hair peeking out from her fur hat.

If someone who didn't know her saw her like this, they might mistake her for some kind of snow spirit instead of a spirit of light. The fur made her look all fluffy, after all.

That said, lately our travels had kept taking us through snowy places--the Empire, the northern Kingdom, and now this--so even I was honestly starting to tire a little of all the silver-white grandeur.

The majesty of nature?

Sure, it was amazing. Beautiful enough to take your breath away. But more than that, right now it was simply hard.

If you asked me what I wanted to see now, I'd say the sea. We'd already seen regular mountains, and we'd already seen snowy scenery. It was about time I saw the ocean. I missed blue skies and the horizon. Come to think of it, I still hadn't gone to the sea even once since coming to this other world.

The sea would be nice.

It wasn't a triathlon, but maybe I should try some long-distance swimming.

That said, while mountain climbing in the snow was boring for the most part, it wasn't as if it had no pleasures at all.

The greatest pleasure. Yes, that was--

--food.

It was cold, and we kept walking.

Which meant we were burning through energy. We had to be consuming a good number of calories too.

In other words, it was a situation where eating a fair bit was allowed.

Even Lumiela had started saying things like, "If we're going to stay in the snowy mountains the whole time... that means I don't have to hold back on food anymore, right...?" with sparkling eyes.

Was she going to be okay? This wasn't about to turn into her suggesting we live in the mountains, was it? That worried me.

To be honest, it was partly my fault that Lumiela's appetite was starting to slip its leash after I'd shown her too many memories of convenience-store fried chicken and the like.

"Lumiela. Let's give dinner our all today."

"Yes, let's! We'll make the best hot pot we've ever had!"

Lumiela's tone jumped all at once. Seeing that smile loosened me up a little too.

We stopped at a place with a good view and looked up toward the sacred peak.

The mountain thrust into the sky so sharply that it seemed like a giant silver pillar piercing the world. Its middle was embraced by a ring of clouds, and its summit dissolved into the heavens themselves, invisible from below. The wind howled, snow blew upward, and the mountain seemed almost to breathe.

Beyond that, the Holy Sword and the sage were waiting.

"It's far..."

"That's probably why it's called a sacred peak..."

Tonight we planned to finish crossing several mountains around the sacred peak, then tomorrow make camp in the conifer forest at the foot of the mountain we would climb next, in a somewhat lower-elevation area.

If we didn't pick a place that blocked at least some of the wind, the night would be hell.

I hacked down a conifer and cut it into convenient lengths.

Each time the blade bit into the frozen wood, a damp, dull sound rang out. Lumiela flicked her fingertip, the heat from her light magic ran through it, and the cut surfaces of the firewood slowly glowed red before a campfire sprang to life.

"There, it's lit."

"I think this every time, but you're really handy. If it were me, I might've erased the wood entirely."

"Heh. I'm a spirit of light."

We lit the fire, arranged stones so the pot would sit stably, and set it on top.

We'd done this enough times now that we'd gotten considerably smoother at it.

It was fun, kind of like camping, but the truth was we'd already failed at cooking a few times.

I'd rather not fail this time.

Thinking that, I started preparing the ingredients.

For food, first came deer meat. Monsters tasted bad, so we'd gone after a real animal.

At noon we'd found huge tracks left in the snowfield, followed them, found the thing that made them, and brought it down.

It had been a giant deer. Maybe because they had to compete with monsters, even animals in this world were bigger and stronger than in my old life. That was good in the sense that there was plenty to eat.

Lunch, incidentally, had been a lazy one.

No, to be precise, maybe I should say I'd given up on making anything.

"We'll use the venison from lunch, and then... hmm."

By the time we'd finished butchering the giant deer at noon, my motivation had died.

Taking out the pot, cutting ingredients, making broth--I had no room left in me for anything so refined. So lunch had simply been the sliced meat grilled as-is and eaten that way. Nothing but steak.

Aside from one little mishap where a stone suddenly went pop and burst like a tiny explosion, it had been perfect.

If you heat certain stones too quickly, they can crack apart from the inside and burst. I didn't know the theory behind it, but I knew it from experience.

In my old life, I hadn't been interested in camping and had never done it, but now I felt a small pang of regret. If I'd gone camping back then, maybe I'd have had a little more knowledge now.

We'd cooked the meat slowly, given it a nice browned crust on the outside, and shared it between the two of us once it was done, but if I was being honest, the taste had been rough.

It was sinewy, and the gamey smell was still there. Still, in the cold and with an empty stomach, maybe that alone qualified as a feast.

But since we were already doing something so much like camping, I'd decided that at least dinner before bed was going to be a serious challenge.

Cooking time, right in the middle of this silver-white world.
