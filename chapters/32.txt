Old Man Rowan (2)







When the old woman finished listening to Rowan, she snorted and slowly shook her head.


"What are you even saying? Violence alone has limits. If you go looking, there are plenty of creatures in this world stronger than you. With that mindset, you'll end up being hammered down as the nail that sticks out. That's how the world works."


The wrinkles in her cheeks trembled like the rings of age built up over a long journey.

And yet her tone carried a sharp edge.

Twisting the corner of his mouth, Rowan answered with a laugh.


"That only applies to ordinary men."


The old woman's brow warped.

He could tell she was about to argue back.


But before she could--

several angry shouts rang out.


"There, you see? What are you going to do now?"


Looking over, Rowan saw reinforcements flooding toward Jane from the scammer's side.

They surrounded him with a barrage of threats, behaving as though their victory was already assured.

And since this was after one battle already, it might have looked like his disciple was in danger.

But--


"Nothing at all."


To Rowan, the outcome was obvious.


"...And yet you call yourself a master. Well, perhaps it's about time he learned firsthand that one person's strength has limits."


"What are you talking about? The boy already understands his own strength. He's not overestimating it. This business too... he's only doing it because he can."


"What are you ev--"


Before she could finish, the old woman turned her gaze back from Rowan toward Jane--

and by then, it was already almost over.

The screams of the reinforcements were echoing out.


From Rowan's perspective, some of those reinforcements were strong.

Against opponents of that level, their methods would usually have worked. That was precisely why the scammers' little operation had continued to function up to now.

Through force.

But this time, there was no other way to put it:

they had chosen the wrong target.


Jane had crushed the reinforcements without so much as blinking.

The old woman could only suck in a breath and stare in astonishment.


Over his long life, Rowan had seen many humans.

There had been strong men among them. Warriors too. Even if not heroes, he'd stood shoulder to shoulder with people of proven skill.


But compared to those days, the era had changed.

More than in the chaotic age through which he had once walked, the humans of the present were far stronger--

that much he could feel in the creeping way it moved beneath the skin.


It was probably adaptation to the environment.

After being exposed for so many years to the threat of demonkind, the human race itself had been tempered.

And not merely tempered. They had been forced to place "fighting" at the center of daily life in order to survive.

That accumulated history had shaped the strength of the present.


The symbol of that was Hero Liam.

Speaking from personal taste, Rowan found Liam's spotless integrity somewhat hard to take. He was too clean, too willing to spill his own blood for the sake of ideals--so clean, in fact, that to Rowan, who had lived in blood and dirt, it was blinding.

Rowan had fought in wars, killed fellow humans, and made choices that had nothing to do with noble purity.

Given a past like that, there was no way he could bring himself to join in teaching such a hero-like young man.


And yet even so, Liam's strength itself could not be denied.

That much Rowan acknowledged honestly.

When Princess Ceras had brought Rowan and the others together with Liam, Rowan had seen the young man's abilities for himself.

He had found himself impressed.

The power of the ice magic, the technique, the physical ability--all of it existed in a realm beyond the ordinary.

When Rowan later heard that Liam had fought one of the Four Heavenly Kings and survived, he found it easy to believe.

Rare indeed was a being who possessed the power worthy of realizing a dreamlike ideal.

One of those who had already reached toward the level where one could stand against the Four Heavenly Kings--

without question, that was Hero Liam.


As for Jane, Rowan did not know what to make of him.

He felt no particular talent for the sword, and yet he'd seen him defeat demon generals and humans alike through overwhelming physical ability.

But it never seemed as though he were seriously fighting.


Which of the two was stronger, Hero Liam or Jane?

Rowan did not know.

It was a domain higher than clouds, beyond sight itself.

He simply did not stand on the same step of understanding needed to grasp that level.


And yet, if the question was one of strangeness, Jane probably won.


There were many kinds of beings called heroes.

Those who moved from goodwill, those who fought for fame, those bound by duty...

But what they all shared was that when evil exceeded a certain point, they rose against it.

If atrocities grew too severe, then a hero made of justice would appear and strike down the villain.

That was the order of this world.

Its cycle.


But Jane fit none of that.


He had not beaten down the scammers and mercenaries out of duty or righteous hatred.

He had done it because they deceived him and tried to kill him.

He struck back because he had been struck at.

That was all.

If he had killed them, people would have criticized it as going too far.

But in reality, he had left them alive.

It was hard to call him truly evil.

At worst, he was a dangerous man too quick to resort to his fists.


He had enough social sense to bring liquor and snacks, and he could be spoken to.

But at the same time, he wielded monstrous power, and if necessary, he looked like the sort who would have no qualms about stabbing from behind.


Their acquaintance was still brief, but Rowan felt he had a rough sense of the man now.


Jane would not commit major evils that would bring ruin upon himself. He would not, for example, seek to destroy the world or massacre humans of his own accord.

And yet neither was he driven by justice, nor by a desire for fame, nor by any lofty philosophy.


He was neither evil nor good.

He was, in a sense, something in the middle.


Though at the moment he leaned slightly toward evil.

No--

perhaps there were times when he leaned toward good as well.

In any case, from society's point of view, he was not that different from a thug.

And heroes did not go around hunting thugs.

The foes heroes truly ought to strike with all their might were demons, savage monsters, and archvillains.

Ordinary citizens too would avoid a thug who might lash out, but they would not bother calling a hero to deal with him. He would simply be treated as a dangerous creature on the edge of society, and that would be the end of it.


There was nothing to gain from defeating Jane.

There was no glory in defeating a man who had simply quarreled with scammers. He was not a threat to the world.

It was not worth the effort.


Ordinarily, the cycle should go like this:

the monster defeated by the hero is feared,

and then the hero who defeats the monster is in turn feared by the people.

But Jane fit neither role.

He was, almost--

a member of the populace with the power of a monster.


He blended into the streets like any ordinary person, making it hard for a hero's eye to catch him.

And yet in truth, he carried within himself monstrous power no common citizen could ever oppose.


Monstrous violence, paired with very human cunning.

That imbalance was exactly what made him so strange.

"What a ridiculous kind of strength... and what a ridiculous way to use it."


After watching the whole affair through to the end, the old woman said that and turned on her heel.


"What, heading back already?"


"Well, heroes like Liam can always use another ally. That's why I came to take a look at your disciple, but..."


"Seems he didn't suit your taste."


"He's not evil. I understand that now. But he could never be a comrade. That's simply the kind of character he is."


Rowan answered that statement with silence, which was as good as agreement.


Jane felt no particular pride in his own power.

As for lofty ideals like using it to protect the weak, Rowan suspected he would throw such things aside the moment the need arose.

It was obvious that he and heroes would eventually come into conflict in some decisive moment.


And yet at the same time, that dirtiness, that rawness, could also be called an extremely human way of living.

That was why Rowan thought:

he did not know what Jane ultimately wished to accomplish.

Maybe he wanted to defeat the Demon King. Maybe he had some completely different objective. Frankly, Rowan did not care.


He simply found himself wanting to teach him.


Jane's technique was ordinary.

He had no special talent.

But his physical strength was absurd.

Combined with that monstrous power, Rowan could easily imagine how explosively he might grow with even a little technical skill.


There were always naturally talented people in the world if one went looking.

Teaching such people was not interesting.

They improved on their own, and someone or another would always end up taking them in anyway.

There was no meaning in Rowan's teaching those sorts.


But no one other than Rowan would want to teach Jane.

And because of that--

Rowan simply wanted, in the purest sense, to see what would happen.
