Rubrum Nox's Side

The technique Atra Ignis, in which black flames spread out and transform the battlefield itself into a scorching zone.

The ground split open in red, rocks melted from within, and the air warped and shimmered. Simply breathing seared the lungs. Against something on the level of a demon general, the instant it stepped inside, it would be carbonized without even being able to scream. It was a domain of flame.

Everything within its range was burned away.

He had left the black dragon Linyuvurm to handle the other enemies. It was not that he did not trust him, but Rubrum Nox had instinctively felt that if they fought here together, the attacks would inevitably catch his partner as well. He had sensed that this would become a fierce battle.

And indeed--
even within this scorching heat that should have reduced all things to ash--
the enemy was still there.

It walked on through the sea of black flames, leaving scorch marks behind. It did not stop moving.

No--from the burned-away parts, other flesh swelled up and shaped itself anew. It was even regenerating. It truly was continuing its life activity.

"--I have never seen a creature like this before."

To living things, fire was terror.

Whenever an enemy stepped into this scorching zone, all of them feared it and required time to steel themselves.

But today's enemies, these three, came plunging in without a moment's pause.

No such creatures should exist in nature.

The monsters writhing before his eyes stank terribly of death.

They felt almost like things formed from piled-up remnants of the dead bodies of living creatures.

The emotions of the dead, crushed, torn apart, trampled. Fear, hatred, regret, screaming. It almost seemed as though such grudges had hardened and entangled in place of flesh.

Had they been dragged up from the bottom of the earth, stitched forcibly together, and then granted power?

Ugly, warped, swelling without limit--perhaps that was what had resulted in those forms.

And that was why they were so strong.

Even so, compared to the human-faced fly he had burned up earlier, these things possessed less of a human element to them. Was that because they were stronger than the fly? Were the monsters they had originally been serving as the base, with the human parts no more than fragments of flesh? Or had they failed to become otherwise?

Rubrum Nox did not understand the logic of that sort of thing. But the strength of it all impressed him in spite of himself--

And then his vision lurched violently.

He had been punched.

The air screamed, and something split with a thunderous report.

A bursting roar battered his eardrums, and his body flew through the air.

As if it felt no pain from being burned, the ogre that had charged ahead of the other two sent him flying with a punch.

Rubrum Nox recovered his balance in midair and tried to land, but by then the ogre had already twisted its mountainlike upper body and was throwing another punch.

There was no option to evade.

Then--
he would take it.

He crossed his arms and braced himself.

The impact was less a blow than a crushing collapse. The air itself compressed, and pain pressed into him in a way he had not felt in a long time.

The ground beneath his planted feet screamed and caved in together with the bedrock. A crushing mass that seemed ready to pulverize his whole body was hammering into him from the front.

Even so, he stopped it.

Keeping it caught, he ground his teeth and pushed power back in return.

It became a contest of strength.

"■■■■■■■------!!!!"

"Ooooooooooooo-----!!!!"

In pure strength, it seemed he had the advantage, and little by little he pushed back. Slowly but surely, the ogre's fist began to retreat.

But--
he could not overpower it.

The muscles along the ogre's surface swelled. Bundles writhed beneath the skin, and more power piled on. Its mass, its density, increased without any will of its own.

The ground beneath them shattered, and Rubrum Nox's body sank in farther. Even if he kept bracing, the ground would reach its limit before he did.

If the ogre were the only opponent, he would not have minded carrying on with a raw contest of strength until one side gave out.

Unfortunately, there were still other enemies. Their presence was what made things troublesome for him.

Just as he thought to use magic and bring black flames to bear--

A red-black wave flooded his vision. Pressure like a tidal wave.

If this were truly some liquid creature, then inside this scorched zone it should already have evaporated.

And yet the liquid life-form continued to exist, held its shape, and came on the offensive all the same.

It might possess some special property. Before he could finish gathering that thought, the wave of red liquid was already trying to swallow his whole body.

Touching it was dangerous.

Rubrum Nox refused to take the full force of the ogre's fist head-on and instead let it slide sideways. He shifted the fulcrum of the collision and diverted its trajectory. The giant arm howled through the air, and the direct hit was avoided.

Immediately afterward, a ground-covering red-black tidal wave rushed in.

There was no time to hesitate. He kicked off the ground and leapt far backward. The place where he had been standing a moment before vanished beneath the torrent of red liquid.

He saw the earth melting away, leaving holes in the ground.

The ogre handled close combat. The liquid creature responded to magic. And as for the poison wolf--
it had begun harrying him from a slight distance away.

Within the heat-warped field of vision, only the abnormal color born from the wolf stood out.

Purple mist. Different from the air rippling in the heat, it spread as though heavy and possessed of will.

He stopped his breathing on instinct.

His instincts were sounding the alarm that he must not inhale it.

Fighting without breathing would certainly weigh down both movement and judgment. He ought to open distance until he was beyond the poison, but the toxic mist was already spreading across the whole area at terrifying speed.

Every place the poison wolf ran through, its entire trail was contaminated, and the avenues of escape had been painted over. On top of that, it was quite fast. Shaking it off would be extremely difficult.

Taken to its logical extreme, he had to assume the poison would remain until he killed the wolf itself.

And poison did not invade only through the lungs. It could enter through skin, wounds, the gaps in armor. It would come in through routes besides the mouth.

At the edge of his vision, the ogre and the liquid creature were standing within the same poisoned zone. But that did not mean there was any reason for relief.

It was doubtful whether the poison even worked on those things.

And so, the fact that he himself was wrapped in this purple fog offered not a single scrap of reassurance.

Then I kill the shield first.

Black flames burst from his back, the recoil driving his whole body forward. He stepped in with that momentum and slammed a full-power right straight into the ogre's jaw.

With a dull shattering sound, the giant body lifted into the air for an instant. Its mountainlike torso bent far backward.

The solid impact remained in his fist. Even so, it was not enough to finish the thing.

He immediately hammered his elbow into its stomach. The difference in body size was troublesome.

Their trinity of coordination came at him without a break.

Because they attacked simultaneously, there were moments when they ended up covering one another.

The instant he thought he had evaded one, something came from behind. So he went on the attack. He drove a fist into the ogre's chest, used the recoil to open distance, and trapped the poison wolf behind a wall of flame to split it off--but even that failed. Perhaps it truly did not feel damage or pain, because it came running back out while burning.

His breath was reaching its limit.

While moving and fighting the ogre and the liquid creature, he waited for the brief interval before the wolf emerged again.

Consciously measuring the distance, he chose the instant when he was as far from the wolf as possible--
and took one short, sharp breath.

He did not need long. He stopped breathing again at once.

But--

Heat filled the back of his nose, and in the next instant something lukewarm trickled down. He realized, a beat late, that it was a nosebleed. Not funny.

Most poisons should not work on Rubrum Nox.
And yet this one was contaminating his body even beyond that resistance.

There could be no mistake.
It was a lethal supertoxin.
