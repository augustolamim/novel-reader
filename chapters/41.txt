"What about this plant...? It's got some weird fruit on it."

"Hm, let me see. ...No good. It has poison in it... and something else harmful too."

Meat alone wasn't enough. I wanted something like mountain vegetables too. And so it was time for Lumiela's inspection service.

The shadows beneath the conifers were deep, and every time the wind passed through, the thin branches and needles rattled hard. As we brushed aside the snow at our feet with numb hands and moved forward through the cold stabbing at our skin, we found--somewhat surprisingly--a few plants poking their heads out.

Apparently they had rooted themselves beneath the frozen ground and were somehow surviving. It was another world, so maybe that was just how things were here.

Their shapes were strangely warped. Long leaves curled inward, their bases swollen. Vegetables... maybe? They were plants, at least. But whether because they had evolved for the cold or for some other reason, they looked nothing like anything ordinary. When I touched one, it felt oddly squishy.

Almost all the plants here were bizarre.

And the vegetation wasn't to be underestimated either. A few days earlier we'd even passed a place where packs of beast-eating plants lay under the snow, suddenly splitting open with a rustling rip and thrusting out sharp thorns from inside. They had been opening and closing mouth-like organs as if waiting for prey.

They had been disgusting, plain and simple.

But precisely because of that, every time we dug through the snow, excitement and caution rose in me at the same time.

This was a remote place, somewhere no humans came near. It wouldn't have been strange at all if there were unknown plants here that were actually delicious. Just thinking about that possibility made my heart jump.

"Ah, this..."

Lumiela crouched down and brushed aside the snow with her fingers.

The moment she cleared it away, a little bit of green lit up against the all-white view.

A round, swollen bud--it looked kind of like a butterbur sprout.

Though this being another world, it was bigger than the butterbur sprouts I'd seen in my previous life. ...Maybe it was only similar and not actually related at all.

Besides, weren't butterbur sprouts something you made tempura out of? With what we had on hand--and with our level of skill--that wasn't happening. Even if it really was one, it wouldn't matter. At best it could go into the hot pot as a stand-in for vegetables.

Carefully pinching the base of the stem, I slowly pulled it up.

"Butterbur-sprout lookalike, acquired."

"Butterbur...? Is that the plant's name?"

"Uh... no idea. It just looks similar..."

"So it's only similar. Then we need to check it."

Lumiela lit a faint glow on her fingertips. It was her magic-based toxicity test.

"...This one is fine. Next..."

"Yeah... the taste. I'll try a little."

I tore off part of the butterbur-sprout lookalike and put it in my mouth.

This was... hmm.

"Feels kind of green and grassy, but it's within acceptable limits. ...About what you'd expect."

"Yes. Once it's in the pot, we probably won't notice."

Apparently Lumiela had tasted a little too, because she offered her verdict.

Our opinions matched.

If it had been something with a strong scent like mint, that would've ruled it out. An herb with too much character could make the whole pot taste strange and throw everything out of balance.

Neither Lumiela nor I was particularly experienced at this, so we were avoiding that kind of thing. What we wanted in our vegetables wasn't anything flashy or oddly flavored. We just wanted something with a decent texture.

After that, we kept searching around the area.

Besides the butterbur-sprout lookalike, we found a few things that resembled plants I'd seen in my previous life, but some of them turned out to be simply poisonous and unusable, so I couldn't exactly call it smooth progress.

Still, repeating little discoveries like that one after another, we slowly increased the amount of safe edible greens we had--

and by the time we'd gathered enough to feel satisfied, the sky had already darkened, the silver-white world had shifted toward crimson, and the falling snow had grown stronger still.

"All right... this should do for today."

Carrying the mountain vegetables we'd gathered, we returned to the place we'd chosen for the night, where we'd made a fire earlier, and built an igloo.

Squatting down, I plunged both arms into the snow.

Thunk.

With a solid resistance, my arms sank into it all the way up to the shoulders, and then I yanked back hard and lifted a huge block of snow free with a heavy drag.

I lightly tossed it to the side just as it was.

After clearing out enough space for the two of us, I piled snow up around us one piece after another to form walls.

With both arms working like bulldozers, I shoved in great amounts of snow, packed it down, and smoothed the surface with sweeping motions until it came out solid and neat.

Once the igloo was finished, I crouched down, leveled the entrance, and slipped inside. The space enclosed by snow was quieter than the outside, and without the wind, it even felt warm by comparison.

I set the venison beside us there, and laid out the mountain vegetables we'd just gathered.

From my bag I also pulled out salt and other seasonings, along with the pot and our tools.

We'd been taking turns cooking, but last night had been Chef Lumiela's creation: a bold masterpiece produced by tossing meat and vegetables into the pot wholesale and stir-frying everything together in one go--"stir-fried meat and vegetables."

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Now that we'd secured a place to sleep, we went back outside the igloo.

We left our packs inside, took only the ingredients we needed for cooking, and carried them out onto the snow. The sky was overcast, and a cold wind passed through now and then.

Just that alone felt like it was stealing away our body heat, and it pushed me with a sense of urgency to make something warm quickly.

"Hey, what should I do next?"

Lumiela asked while lighting a small glow on her fingertips.

"Warm the melted snow. Just short of boiling is fine."

"Leave it to me."

Lumiela held out her hand and wrapped a pale film of light around the pot of snowmelt. The surface of the water slowly stirred, and before long steam began to rise from it.

Meanwhile, I turned back to the lump of meat. The fat had frozen white and hard as stone, but when I held it near the fire, it gradually began to melt with a sizzle.

The smell of melting fat tickled my nose, and my empty stomach grumbled its complaint.

Next I lined up the butterbur-sprout lookalikes and a few round root-vegetable-like things we'd just gathered on the cutting board. When I put the knife to them, they offered a slightly firm resistance.

Their cross-sections were a pale green, with a faint earthy scent.

"The water's looking good."

Lumiela glanced back. Soft white steam drifted up from the pot. It looked warm.

After all our trial and error so far, we'd more or less figured out how to divide the work.

I had Lumiela handle the finer preparation--checking for poison and warming cold water to just the right degree, that sort of thing.

Honestly, I couldn't do something like adjusting water to the proper temperature. Since it wasn't fire magic, it was beyond me. I had tried, and all I'd managed to do was evaporate the water.

It was probably some applied use of the heat light gave off, but controlling the output within such a fine range had to be an advanced technique.

Without hesitation, I dumped in the chopped vegetables. Then I tore the half-thawed meat into small pieces and dropped those into the pot too, and at once the scent rising with the steam grew stronger, filling the snowy world with air that felt almost absurdly warm.

The bubbling sound of the pot scratched pleasantly at my ears.

I took out the bag of salt, pinched some between my fingers, and sprinkled it in. The grains flashed like little stars over the surface.

One pinch. Hmm, maybe that's not enough.

Another pinch. ...Should I go farther?

And then one more.

"A salt soup loaded with ingredients... or was it a salt hot pot? This makes the third time since we started climbing snowy mountains. I wonder what it'll taste like this time."

Lumiela let out a small laugh. It felt strange that she still sounded happy when this was our third try and she ought to have been tired of the flavor by now.

She had almost called it salt soup, but since I kept calling it a salt hot pot, Lumiela had started correcting herself the same way.

Soup sounded like something secondary, so I wanted to insist this was a hearty hot pot.

Still... the first time, the flavor had been way too strong. The second time, maybe because we'd added vegetables with too much personality, it had failed again. So far, this had not been going especially well.

Lumiela had eaten it without complaint both times, but I got the feeling she'd been sparing my feelings, and that was a bit...

I had considered trying an entirely different menu, but some part of me still felt like maybe, just maybe, third time's the charm.

There was nothing for it. It wasn't as if I didn't want to use a convenient cooking cheat and feed Lumiela something truly delicious, but...

"Third time, huh. Well, it can't be helped... In the early days, whenever we tried making something too fancy, we ended up with disaster food. For people like us who don't know much about cooking, the things we can make out here are basically simple dishes only... venison steak, raw vegetables, stir-fried meat and vegetables, and hot water with meat, vegetables, and what I hope is the right amount of salt..."

"Part of it is the seasoning issue. We can only carry so much. And there's a limit to what we can gather locally too, so it can't be helped. From here on, it's a matter of ingenuity and getting used to it."

"Getting used to it, huh. Maybe we'll pull it off this time. Even the salt level."

I scooped up a little to taste it. Still weak. I set some salt on my fingertips and this time let it fall lightly into the center of the pot. The broth rippled, and the previously clear soup took on the faintest bit of color.

"Oh."

When I tasted it, the salt was just right. Compared to the last two times, this might actually be overwhelmingly more complete.
