Episode 122: Snacks








After leaving the liquor store and passing through the street, the next place we stopped was a small shop that sold drinking snacks.

The exterior had stone walls and a wooden signboard, and when I opened the door, the scents of dried herbs and smoked meat softly tickled my nose. The shelves were lined with aged cheese, lamb marinated in herbs, salted nuts, and amber-colored bottles of olive oil.

When I asked here too whether they had any recommendations--


"Something to go with liquor? Then this is the one."


The snack-shop owner pointed at a thinly sliced reddish-brown block.


"Karasumi."


"...Karasumi?"


I tilted my head at the unfamiliar word.


"Fish roe packed in salt and dried in the wind. Liquor's best companion. Some connoisseurs say they can't even get drunk properly without it."


I nodded in understanding, but frowned slightly.


'Fish roe... that's an unusual food. I wonder what it tastes like.'


'I'm pretty sure I ate it once before... but I guess that just means I ate it and forgot the taste.'


Karasumi...

I had the feeling I had heard of it before.

For a moment, a memory from my previous life brushed the edge of my mind. I was pretty sure it had been eaten as a delicacy in some coastal country. I thought I had bought it as a souvenir for someone when I traveled abroad once...

I must have tried a sample then, but I couldn't remember what it tasted like.

I had a feeling it had been expensive, though.


According to the owner, it was a preserved food that took a great deal of work: fish roe salted and dried.


"Here, have a little taste."


Saying that, the owner carefully sliced off a piece with a small knife.

Each time he cut it thin, oil clung thickly to the blade and tugged it back slightly.


He placed the thin slice on a small plate and held it out.

I gingerly took one piece and broke it in half.

The moment I did, Lumiela swiftly snatched one half and popped it into her mouth.

I slid my mask up just enough to quickly put the remaining half into my own mouth.

After a moist texture on the tongue, saltiness and a distinctive depth of flavor spread through my mouth.

Still...

it was a little strong to eat by itself.

Come to think of it, this did feel like the taste I remembered.


'...That's a pretty particular flavor.'


'It's very salty...'


'Well, yeah... tastes like the kind of thing people would either really like or not, but apparently it's popular, so maybe people who like alcohol are into it.'


While we were talking through telepathy, the owner, probably thinking we'd gone silent for too long while tasting it, leaned forward.

And then he started talking.

And talking.

Apparently he was absolutely in love with karasumi.


"You can only make it in coastal port towns. You carefully salt the fish ovaries and dry them in the sun. Even that takes weeks. It takes work, patience, and if you get the temperature or humidity wrong, the whole batch is ruined. But if you succeed, you get this deep flavor."


I listened to the karasumi-loving owner explain all this with a repeated, "I see?"

There was a fervor to the way he spoke that bordered on religious devotion.


"Lately, though, supplies are running short. Ever since those demon bastards attacked, every workshop I traded with shut down. They said they didn't want to work while there was a chance they might die."


The owner's voice dropped a little.


"If the number of makers falls, then naturally the price rises. But plenty of people still buy it anyway, no matter the cost. Escapism, you know? They drink liquor like they're drowning in it, and pick up delicacies while they're at it. With faces like tomorrow isn't coming. Honestly, it's a pain. A real pain."


And yet even as he said that, he looked exasperated and smiled.


'So what do we do? Are we buying it?'


'Hmm... honestly, I don't know. Would Old Man Rowan even eat karasumi?'


'If he rejects it, I guess we'll have to eat it ourselves.'


'...Yeah. If he says he doesn't want it, we'll eat it.'


'It's salty, but I'm sure we'll manage somehow.'


'Come to think of it, I think if you cook it with pasta or something, it becomes karasumi pasta and tastes good... apparently.'


While we exchanged that silly little conversation through telepathy, I ended up buying one wrapped portion of karasumi after all.

It was wrapped in stiff parchment and tied with hemp string. I took it, paid the owner, and immediately noticed that it really was expensive.

Maybe it really counted as a luxury ingredient.




--------------------------------------------------




Before leaving, I asked the owner who loved karasumi whether he had any recommendations other than karasumi.

At first he kept insisting karasumi was supreme, but when I asked what his number two would be--what he would suggest aside from that--he stroked his chin and finally answered.


"Karasumi is the untouchable number one... but if you're pairing something with wine, then cheese and pickles would be next. Especially with white wine, the saltiness and acidity hit just right."


Cheese and...

pickles.

Cheese, sure. But pickles...


He told us, "Cheese is the main thing... but if you want, there's also a place around the corner past the street that specializes in pickles too." After thanking him, we stepped back outside.


Then, between the red-roofed buildings, I spotted a small signboard.


When we went inside, the sweet scent of milk spread softly through the air. The shelves were lined with cheeses of all shapes and sizes: yellow ones, white ones, even some with blue mold.


I called out to the female owner and asked what would go well with wine. She immediately nodded with a smile and began looking over the cheeses in thought.


While she was doing that, I searched on my own for the pickles and noticed jars lined up on a high shelf.

Pickles.

Remembering what the karasumi-loving owner had said, I grabbed a small jar while I was at it.


"The cheeses that go well with wine are around here."


The female owner pointed toward several types of cut cheese arranged on a small wooden board. White ones, pale yellow ones, and some with lightly aged rinds.

Each of them gave off its own distinct aroma, so much so that the air in that corner felt denser than the rest of the shop.


'Cheese... it looks delicious!'


Lumiela wasn't sparing the pickles even a glance. Her eyes were fixed on the cheese.

Well, to be fair, the cheese really did look delicious.

For our own share, I bought quite a lot of cheese, plus the pickles too.


When we stepped back outside, the day had started to warm up.

The temperature was rising, and it was clear noon was drawing near.


I gave my body a light stretch.


'All right. Let's head back.'


'We got a proper break in too, so once we're out of town, I'll run again.'


Lumiela gently stopped floating and set her feet on the ground.

She landed without so much as a footstep, light as a feather.

But her face was full of determination. She tied her hair back in one bundle and gave a little hop in place.


As we walked, the number of people passing by gradually increased.

The shops were opening one after another. It felt as though we were standing right there at the moment the sleeping town woke up.


That made me a little sentimental.

Karasumi, cheese, pickles.

Maybe it was because the lineup reminded me of my previous life.


Before long, under the sun climbing toward noon, the people moving through town would surely start walking faster too.

Watching them, I made a quiet decision deep inside.

Once we left town, we'd run.

As if to confirm the beginning of this day in the wind.
