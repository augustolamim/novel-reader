Old Man Rowan (1)



Rowan's Side






A man stood alone, unscathed, in the center of what had become a heap of rubble.

A suspicious figure in a mask and hood.


All around him lay burly men sprawled out with the whites of their eyes showing, as if a storm had swept through.

Huge mercenaries with thick muscles had fallen--

and not one of them showed the slightest sign of getting back up.


The people of Area 1, drawn by the commotion, did nothing more than peer in fear.

Not one of them dared come closer.

Even those who had been shouting only moments ago, "What the hell's all this noise?!", now merely swallowed hard as their legs failed them.


Amid all that, Rowan stood there with complicated feelings he could not quite put into words.

He was supposed to be watching over his disciple's practice that afternoon, but the morning had been free.

He had merely intended to wander aimlessly on a stroll.

He had not expected to walk straight into a sight like this.

Lately, with his disciple bringing him liquor and snacks, he had been living at ease without lacking for either.

He had fully expected to spend his time lazily.


And yet, to think he would stumble upon that very disciple rampaging on a grand scale...

not even Rowan had imagined such a thing.


The strength of it all left him half exasperated, half amused.

From the moment they met, that disciple--Jane--had already been strong.

A disciple stronger than his master from the very beginning was unheard of, and yet Jane possessed strength so far beyond reason that "invincible" almost seemed the right word.


Even looking with the eyes of a nominal master, Rowan could not glimpse the bottom of him.

If he tried to peer into it, all that seemed to spread before him was a darkness deeper still--

an endless abyss with no end in sight.


That strange bond by which Rowan had taken such a being as a disciple had begun from the smallest of accidents.


He had known from their first meeting.

That hidden strength would simply crush anything and everything through sheer physical might.

He would laugh at the very concept of technique, ignore differences in training and experience alike.

He wore the shape of a man, but in truth he was a monster beyond human comprehension.


And precisely because of that--

because of that, a certain curiosity had taken root in Rowan.

What kind of reaction would this monster have if Rowan showed him his art?

It was simple morbid curiosity.

Ordinarily one should have avoided such a thing, but Rowan was old now. Compared to merely aging away, poking at the beast seemed more appealing.

And alcohol had certainly played a part too.


To show off his skill with a liquor bottle, he created a little bit of trickery disguised as art and deliberately provoked him.

He could easily imagine what might happen if one provoked a monster like that.

If Jane decided he had been looked down on, then Rowan was nothing more than an old drunk with technique.

He might literally be crushed in the man's grip.

And yet Rowan had been fine with that.

He had lived without regrets and believed he could accept it even if he were crushed.

At least, he had believed so at the time.


He had probably just been drunk.

Ever since he was young, drink had a way of making him do something idiotic and regret it later.


And then the most unexpected thing of all had happened.

After Rowan showed his technique and joked, half in jest, "How about I give you some guidance as your master?", the monster had actually nodded.


Rowan had simply stopped thinking.

He barreled ahead on pure momentum, and in the end had even gone with him into Areas 2 and 3, half wondering if maybe he had already been beaten half to death and was seeing hallucinations.


Impossible.

No--

it had happened anyway.

That night, Rowan had buried his head in his hands over the way he had acted that day.


The monster--Jane--had wanted technique.

He possessed overwhelming violence, yet was unsatisfied with that alone.

That was why he wanted skill.


When Rowan heard that, he furrowed his brow.

Wouldn't a monster, by its very nature, be stronger if it simply rampaged with raw force rather than bothering with half-measures and clever tricks?

Reason and strategy would be unnecessary.

Crush, smash, overwhelm.

That was what a monster ought to do best, and perhaps too much cunning would only weigh it down.


But that was not the case.

No--

something more fundamental than that was different.


He was not a monster.

He was human.

He schemed like a human, and at times could be cold, underhanded, even petty.

At the same time, he also possessed the sort of absolute violence one would call monstrous.

He was a strange being who held both sides at once.


Jane did not have even the slightest trace of arrogance.

No matter how much power he held, no matter how monstrous his fighting style might be, he always acted while thinking about how to kill the opponent while suffering as little harm himself as possible.

If that was the case, then there was no contradiction at all.

If he was not half-committed, but instead fundamentally the sort who used tricks as readily as strength.

"What a mess... So that's your disciple, is it?"


As Rowan watched the store reduced to rubble with the detachment of a man enjoying it as drinking entertainment, a familiar rasping voice sounded beside him.

Turning, he found there a bent old woman--

an old acquaintance, once a mage who had dominated battlefields with overwhelming magic.


"What is it, hag? You got a complaint?"


"Oh, plenty. Nothing but complaints. Didn't the princess say to teach your skills to the hero?"


She had once been beautiful in her youth, but now her speaking to him brought no joy. He remembered the irritating face, though not the name anymore. To him, she was simply an old hag.


"Did she now? As I recall it, she said to pass them on to someone strong."


The old woman scowled and spat out her answer.


"Even so, you should choose the person. It's bad news if those techniques get passed on to him. Look at him. He's trying to kill that man."


Indeed, Jane had the shopkeeper in one hand, lifting him clear off the ground.

The man could do little more than kick weakly, entirely unable to resist.


"That remains to be seen."


No sooner had Rowan muttered that than his disciple suddenly raised his voice for all around to hear.


"Unforgivable! You scammer! You tried to swap my gem out for a fake in the back to make a profit! And not only that, you even hired mercenaries to kill me! What a despicable shop! I only struck back because I had no choice! This really is the worst kind of place!"


His voice echoed through the narrow streets of Area 1.

The onlookers exchanged glances and began nodding one after another.

As if to say:

so the shop was in the wrong.


Beside him, the old woman stared blankly, eyes wide in disbelief.

Rowan could not hold it in and burst out laughing.


"Kukuku... hahaha!"


That was right.

That was an appeal.

He was declaring that he was not evil--

or more precisely, that he was not the sort of existence society itself should turn against.

This happened for a reason.

I am not the guilty one.

That was the shape he was trying to give the scene.


And yet, at its core, it looked to Rowan like an animal baring its fangs desperately to protect its own pack.

Clothed in the blood of prey, threatening the world around him--

perhaps that really was fitting for the boss of a pack.
